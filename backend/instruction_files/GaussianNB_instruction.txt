OBSERVED_MODEL_BEHAVIOR:
•  accuracy improves steeply from 1 to 3 features (mean: 0.671 → 0.865), with the largest jump at size 3
•  standard deviation remains high even at 3 features (0.112 vs 0.083 for CatBoost, 0.096 for DecisionTree) — GaussianNB is less stable overall
•  GaussianNB assumes feature independence and Gaussian distributions — violated assumptions cause sharper accuracy drops than in tree or boosting models
•  performs best when selected features are approximately normally distributed and statistically independent
•  highly sensitive to class imbalance because prior probabilities are learned from unbalanced data without correction
•  categorical features cannot be naturally modelled by GaussianNB's Gaussian likelihood — even with encoding, categorical subsets consistently underperform numerical ones
•  the widest accuracy range of all observed models (0.14–1.00) reflects the model's strong assumption dependency

MODEL_SELECTION_REASONING:
GaussianNB should be prioritised when features are approximately Gaussian-distributed, the dataset is balanced, and all or nearly all selected features are numerical. It performs strongly on compact high-signal datasets (5–9 features) and large numerical datasets (30+ features) where high-signal subsets can be identified. It is the fastest model to train and infer, making it suitable for baseline probabilistic classification and resource-constrained environments, provided data assumptions are met.

MODEL_AVOIDANCE_REASONING:
Avoid GaussianNB for imbalanced datasets — it suffers the steepest accuracy penalty (~20.7%) of all compared models and without prior correction its posteriors are distorted by class frequency. Also avoid for categorical-dominant feature subsets, mixed numerical-categorical selections, or datasets where feature independence and Gaussian distribution assumptions are clearly violated (e.g., ordinal, skewed, or sparse features). For noisy or assumption-violating data, DecisionTreeClassifier or CatBoostClassifier are more robust alternatives.
